Violent death in America, drawn from the National Violent Death Reporting System.

NVDRS is the CDC's case-level ledger of violent death in the United States. What follows are the patterns it records — who dies, how, when, and in what circumstances.

Scope. NVDRS covers violent deaths only: suicide, homicide, legal intervention, unintentional firearm, and deaths of undetermined intent. It does not cover disease mortality, motor-vehicle accidents, or accidental drug overdoses. All figures below are for 2021, the most recent year with a complete published surveillance summary, covering 48 states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico.

06 — Timing

Seasonal and weekly patterns.

Suicide and homicide follow distinct and largely opposite temporal rhythms. Indexes below: 100 = annual average.

Suicide, by month

Suicides peak in April and May and are lowest in December. The "holiday suicide" assumption is not supported by the data; the spring peak has been documented in U.S. mortality data since the 19th century.

Homicide, by month

Homicides peak in July and are lowest in February. Summer rates run roughly 35% above winter rates.

08 — Murder-suicide

Linked homicide-suicide incidents.

NVDRS captures incidents in which the perpetrator kills one or more victims and then themselves. These account for roughly 2–3% of homicides and 3% of suicides, and are typically excluded from both headline categories.

Factors cited in the research literature

Firearm prevalence. Rocky Mountain states have the highest household firearm-ownership rates in the country. Firearm suicide attempts have an estimated case-fatality rate above 85%, compared with under 5% for drug overdose.
Rurality and access to care. Low population density is associated with longer distances to psychiatric services and fewer mental-health providers per capita.
Altitude. County-level studies (e.g., Brenner et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2014) find a positive association between elevation and suicide rate after controlling for firearms, rurality, and demographics. The proposed mechanism is chronic mild hypoxia affecting serotonin metabolism. The association is consistent but the causal pathway remains debated.

Public mass shootings account for roughly 3% of U.S. homicides and under 1% of all violent deaths recorded by NVDRS. Definitions vary: the Gun Violence Archive's 4+-injured count yields higher totals; the FBI's active-shooter definition yields lower ones. 700 is a mid-range annual figure over the past decade.

Mass-shooting deaths are not separately tabulated in the NVDRS surveillance summary; the figure here is drawn from the Violence Project and Gun Violence Archive databases.

Methodology & sources

Primary source. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS), Surveillance Summary for 2021 (MMWR SS, published 2024). Covers 48 participating states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico.

Rate calculations. Demographic and state rates per 100,000 are drawn from CDC WISQARS (Web-based Injury Statistics Query and Reporting System) using 2021 final mortality data. Veteran suicide rate from the VA 2023 National Veteran Suicide Prevention Annual Report (2021 data). State rates are age-adjusted per CDC convention.

What NVDRS does not cover. NVDRS excludes unintentional deaths (motor-vehicle crashes, accidental overdoses, drownings), disease mortality, and fatal medical events. "Violent death" in this document refers specifically to deaths from intentional or undetermined violence.

Circumstance coding. NVDRS circumstance fields are coded by trained state abstractors. Circumstances are contributing rather than sole causes; a single case commonly has multiple coded circumstances, so percentages do not sum to 100.

State participation. Florida and Hawaii were not fully reporting to NVDRS in 2021. Their absence modestly suppresses national totals. The true U.S. violent-death figure is higher than the 70,688 shown.

Rounding. Percentages are rounded to the nearest whole number except where a decimal is shown for category shares. Per-day and per-minute figures are derived from the 70,688 annual total.

Crisis resources. In the U.S., call or text 988 to reach the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, 24/7.
