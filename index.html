A DATA PORTRAIT Source: CDC NVDRS, 2021 surveillance data

The shape of violent death
in America.

Every year, tens of thousands of Americans die not from disease or accident, but from a gun, a rope, a blade, a dose. The National Violent Death Reporting System records them one by one. Here is what the ledger shows.

What this shows. The National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS) is the CDC's most detailed ledger of violent death in the United States. It is the only federal dataset that links death certificates, coroner reports, and police narratives for each case. It does not cover all deaths — cancer, heart disease, car crashes, drug overdoses ruled accidental — only deaths caused by intentional or undetermined violence. Figures below are for 2021, the most recent year with a complete published surveillance summary, covering 48 states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico.

The popular image of American violence is a homicide — a stranger, a street, a crime. The data tell a different story. For every American killed by someone else, nearly two kill themselves. Suicide is the quiet mass casualty event running underneath every news cycle.

If violent deaths happened on a clock, one American would die this way every 7 minutes, 26 seconds — all day, all night, all year.

The gun is the through-line.

06 / When

The calendar of American violence.

If you were guessing when Americans are most likely to kill themselves, you would probably say December, alone at the holidays. You would be wrong. Death has its own season — and it runs opposite to the one you would expect.

SUICIDE, by month (index: 100 = annual average)

Peak: April and May. Low: December. The "holiday suicide" cliché is the most durable myth in mental-health folklore. Researchers call the real pattern the Sylvia Plath effect: when energy returns faster than hope, a depressed person becomes dangerous to themselves.

HOMICIDE, by month (index: 100 = annual average)

Peak: July. Low: February. Heat, open windows, school being out, alcohol, and people simply being outside together push summer homicides 35% above winter. The curve is inverted from suicide's.

And then the week.

SUICIDE by day of week
HOMICIDE by day of week

Suicide is a Monday morning. Homicide is a Saturday night. Violent death in America has a weekly rhythm — and that rhythm is, quietly, a piece of public-health intelligence we refuse to use.

07 / Where it actually happens

The most dangerous room in America is a kitchen.

Pop culture codes homicide as an alley, a parking lot, a stranger in a hoodie. NVDRS codes something else entirely. The typical American homicide scene is a living room. The typical killer is on the lease.

"Stranger danger" is the least statistically accurate lesson in American public safety. For a woman killed in the United States, the person most likely to do it is not a thief or a psychopath. It is her husband or boyfriend, in the house they share, with a gun that was bought for protection.

08 / A hidden category

Murder-suicide.

Every two to three days, an American kills a family member and then themselves. These deaths rarely make national news and don't fit neatly into either headline category — so they are the American violence no one counts.

Each dot is one murder-suicide incident in the United States, 2021. Roughly 650 incidents, ~1,400 deaths.

The fastest-rising subcategory is not what you would guess. It is men 65 and older — often caregivers whose spouse has dementia or terminal illness. Researchers reach for the phrase "mercy killing." NVDRS investigators note that the victim almost never consented. The deaths cluster in bedrooms, and they cluster in states with easy firearm access.

09 / The suicide belt

A band of death runs down the Rockies.

Draw a line from the Mexican border to Canada down the spine of the Rocky Mountains. You have traced the worst stretch of American suicide. Epidemiologists call it the Suicide Belt — and its boundaries are strangely precise.

Three forces converge here.

I.
Firearms. Rocky Mountain states lead the country in household gun ownership. Firearms are the most lethal suicide method — roughly 90% of attempts are fatal, compared to 3% for poisoning. When a gun is in the house, impulse becomes permanence.
II.
Isolation. Low population density means longer drives to crisis services, weaker social surveillance, and a cultural premium on self-reliance. The nearest psychiatric inpatient bed can be 200 miles away.
III.
Altitude. Here is the genuinely strange finding: studies of U.S. county-level data consistently find additional suicides per 100,000 for every 2,000 feet of elevation, even after controlling for guns, rurality, and demographics. The leading hypothesis is chronic mild hypoxia disrupting serotonin metabolism. The Mountain West is geologically primed for despair.

10 / Context

What mass shootings are, in the ledger.

Mass shootings are real. They are uniquely American. And in NVDRS's ledger of death, they are a very small fraction of the overall violence. The attention they command is inversely proportional to the deaths they cause.

Every square = 100 American homicide deaths, 2021
Non-mass-shooting homicide (≈20,500)
Public mass-shooting deaths (≈700)

About 700 Americans die in public mass shootings in a typical year — roughly 3% of homicides, a small fraction of 1% of all violent deaths. The remaining 97% are not mass events. They are one-on-one. They are intimate. They are domestic. They are, overwhelmingly, a man with a gun and a grievance, in a kitchen.

Definitions vary. The Gun Violence Archive's broader mass-shooting count (4+ injured) yields higher totals; the FBI's active-shooter definition yields lower ones. 700 is a mid-range annual figure for public fatal mass shootings over the past decade.

Mass shootings dominate our attention. Single shootings dominate our morgues. A policy that stopped every mass shooting tomorrow would still leave ninety-seven percent of American gun homicide — and one hundred percent of firearm suicide — exactly where it is today.

Every violent death in America is a data point. Every data point was, twenty-four hours earlier, a person making coffee.

Methodology & sources

Primary source. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS), Surveillance Summary for 2021 (MMWR SS, published 2024). Covers 48 participating states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico. Figures marked "2021" are drawn directly from this surveillance summary.

Rate calculations. Demographic rates per 100,000 come from CDC WISQARS (Web-based Injury Statistics Query and Reporting System) using 2021 final mortality data. Veteran suicide rate from the VA 2023 National Veteran Suicide Prevention Annual Report (2021 data). State rates are age-adjusted per CDC convention.

What NVDRS is not. NVDRS does not capture unintentional deaths (car crashes, accidental overdoses, drownings), disease mortality, or fatal medical events. "How Americans die" in this graphic means specifically: how Americans die by intentional or undetermined violence.

Circumstance data. NVDRS circumstance fields are coded by trained state abstractors reading law-enforcement reports, medical-examiner narratives, and toxicology. Circumstances are contributing, not sole, causes — a single death commonly has multiple coded circumstances, so percentages do not sum to 100.

State participation. Two states (Florida and Hawaii) were not fully reporting to NVDRS in 2021; their absence modestly suppresses national totals. The real U.S. violent-death figure is somewhat higher than the 70,688 shown.

Rounding. All percentages are rounded to the nearest whole number except where a decimal is shown for category shares. Deaths-per-day and per-minute figures are derived from the 70,688 annual total.

Crisis resources. In the U.S., call or text 988 to reach the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, 24/7.

Data: CDC NVDRS 2021 · CDC WISQARS 2021 · VA 2023 Veteran Suicide Report. Visualization built April 2026.
